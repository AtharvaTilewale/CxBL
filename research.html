The Architect & The Builder

Think of developing a new protein like constructing a skyscraper.

1. Drafting the Blueprints

Before laying a single brick, an architect uses computer software to design the building, testing if it will stand up to wind and earthquakes. In our lab, we use supercomputers to design proteins and simulate how they move and interact.

2. Construction & Testing

Once the blueprints are ready, the builders take over. They pour concrete and assemble beams. We do this in the "wet lab," using bacteria as tiny factories to build the actual proteins we designed on the computer.

If the building (protein) doesn't work as planned, we go back to the blueprints and improve the design.

The Iterative Design Cycle

Integrating High-Performance Computing with Biochemical Validation.

Phase 1: In Silico Design

  • Target identification and structure retrieval (PDB/AlphaFold2).
  • Rational design using Rosetta or machine learning models (e.g., ProteinMPNN).
  • Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations (GROMACS, 500ns+) to assess conformational stability and RMSD fluctuations.

Phase 2: In Vitro Validation

  • Gene synthesis and cloning into expression vectors (e.g., pET-28a).
  • Recombinant expression in E. coli BL21(DE3).
  • Protein purification via Ni-NTA affinity chromatography.

Phase 3: Characterization

  • Enzyme kinetics determining $K_m$, $k_{cat}$, and catalytic efficiency ($k_{cat}/K_m$).
  • Thermostability assessment using Circular Dichroism (CD) spectroscopy and DSC.
  • Data feeds back into computational models for the next design round.
